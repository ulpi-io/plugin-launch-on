# Human writing rules

Source: [blader/humanizer](https://github.com/blader/humanizer), version 2.9.1 (MIT), based on Wikipedia's *Signs of AI writing* guide.

Apply these rules in embedded mode to every user-facing prose artifact: listings, profiles, pitches, outreach, social posts, submission answers, plans that will be shared externally, and any copy generated for a launch.

## Non-negotiable process

1. Preserve the information, not the original shape. Compress, merge, or split as needed.
2. Never invent facts, names, numbers, dates, quotes, citations, features, metrics, customers, or outcomes. If needed detail is unavailable, use a plain version or ask.
3. Match the intended voice. A user-provided writing sample outranks these style preferences.
4. Scan for clusters of the patterns below, rewrite, then audit: "What makes this obviously AI generated?" and "Did I add any unsupported fact?" Revise again before returning copy.
5. Unless a supplied voice sample calls for them, remove em dashes, en dashes, and double hyphens used as dashes. Do not change code, frontmatter, structured data, link targets, quotations, titles, or proper names merely because they contain a watched phrase.

## Patterns to remove

1. Inflated significance, legacy, pivotal-moment, or broader-trend claims.
2. Name-dropping and unspecific notability or media-coverage claims.
3. Superficial analysis added through trailing `-ing` phrases.
4. Promotional or advertisement-like language, including "vibrant", "breathtaking", "renowned", "must-visit", and "groundbreaking" when unsupported.
5. Vague attributions such as "experts believe" or "industry reports". Name a real source or cut the claim.
6. Formulaic "challenges and future prospects" padding and generic outlook sections.
7. Clusters of AI vocabulary: additionally, delve, crucial, enhance, fostering, highlight, intricate, landscape, pivotal, showcase, tapestry, testament, underscore, vibrant.
8. Needlessly indirect copula avoidance: prefer simple "is", "are", and "has" over "serves as", "stands as", "boasts", and "features" where clearer.
9. Negative parallelisms and tailing negations: "not just X, but Y", "no guessing", and similar constructions.
10. Forced lists of exactly three items.
11. Synonym cycling when repeating the clearest term is better.
12. False ranges of unrelated endpoints, such as "from X to Y".
13. Passive voice or subjectless fragments when naming the actor makes the statement clearer.
14. Em/en dashes and dash-separated asides, unless the approved voice sample uses them at that frequency.
15. Mechanical boldface overuse.
16. Vertical lists with bold inline headers that can be clear prose or a plain list.
17. Title Case headings; use sentence case unless the platform convention requires otherwise.
18. Decorative emojis.
19. Curly quotes when the destination does not require them.
20. Chatbot artifacts and offer-to-continue closers: "I hope this helps", "let me know", "would you like", "here is an overview".
21. Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers and speculative gap-filling. State only what is known.
22. Sycophantic or servile responses, including generic praise of the reader's question or idea.
23. Filler phrases: "in order to", "due to the fact that", "at this point in time", "it is important to note".
24. Stacked hedges such as "could potentially possibly".
25. Generic upbeat conclusions and empty send-offs. End on a concrete fact or plan.
26. Unnatural uniform hyphenation of common word pairs, especially after the noun.
27. Persuasive authority tropes: "the real question", "at its core", "what really matters", "the heart of the matter".
28. Signposting announcements: "let's dive in", "here's what you need to know", "without further ado".
29. Fragmented headings followed by a one-line restatement of the heading.
30. Diff-anchored prose that narrates a change instead of describing the current thing, except in inherently versioned documents.
31. Manufactured punchlines and runs of staccato dramatic fragments.
32. Aphorism formulas that sound profound without making a precise claim.
33. Fake-candid rhetorical openers such as "Honestly?", "Look," and "Here's the thing".

## False-positive guard

Do not flatten legitimate formal/technical language, one isolated transition or em dash, intentional quotations, source examples, or a human writer's specific details, mixed feelings, genuine asides, and varied cadence. Look for patterns in combination. Keep the correct register for technical, legal, reference, or platform-required copy; personality is useful only when the author and context call for it.
